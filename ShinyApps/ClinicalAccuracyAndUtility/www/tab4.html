How to read graph 2: clinical utility of a diagnostic test

Graph 2 illustrates how decisions can be made rationally using information from a diagnostic test.

A rational decision is one based on the clinical utility (i.e. the net benefits, harms, and costs) expected to follow from that decision.

The clinical utility of a positive test result depends on (i) the net benefit for people who have a true positive result, and (ii) on the net benefit for people who have a false positive result.

Similarly, the clinical utility of a negative test result depends on (i) the net benefit for people who have a true negative result, and (ii) on the net benefit for people who have a false negative result.

So, on balance for a group of people, their average clinical utility for a positive result depends on the true and false postive rates of the test, and for a negative result on true and false negative rates of the test.

Thus, the first step to assessing clinical utility is to understand the factors that determine decision-making. This is quite tricky, but Graph 2 can help. The aim is to gain an intuitive understanding of the predictive values of a test (the probabilities of the condition being present when a test is positive, or being absent when the test is negative), how these depend on the prevalance (prior probability) of the condition, and how treatment decisions depend, not simply on the result, but on the rates of true and false results.

The explantions below require careful reading and exploration of the interactive graph, but this will be be repaid by the solid understanding that will result.

The X-axis

Did you notice that there is no X-axis!

Although the graph might seem to be a normal X-Y plot, what looks like an X-axis is a visual metaphor showing how test results move from pre-test probability on the left to post-test probability on the right.

This is why what looks like the X-axis has no scale.

The pre-test probability (prevalence) and the post-test probabilities (predictive values)

The green horizontal line at y = 0.4 shows the pre-test probability of the condition, its prevalence, of 40%.

The salmon pink line starting at the prevalence rate and ending at the positive predictive value (PPV) of 75% shows how a positive result increases the probability of the condition.

Where do I find the data?

Data for your condition's prevalence and the test's accuracy (sensitivity and specificity) will be in the scientific literature, unless the test is under development, in which case unpublished data from the developer may be available.Use data from a (well conducted and well reported) systematic review and meta-analysis, if this is available. Otherwise, use data from a (well conducted and well reported) study. Data for the population size should be available from the the source of your accuracy data.

Obtaining reliable data for the decision thresholds can be a challenge. But it is a challenge worth rising to, as the decision thresholds determine the clinical utility of the test.

Health economists do the kind of studies that determine the decision threshold that provide the optimum cost for net benefit --- net benefit takes account of benefits and the harms. Costs, benefits, and harms are those that follow from doing the test and acting on its result.

In technical terms, the recipe that the health economists folllow is:

Decision threshold = (net harm)/(net harm + net benefit)
Net harm = utility(no Rx | no disease) - utility(treatment | no disease)
Net benefit = utility(Rx | disease) - utility(no treatment | disease)ease)

Because this type of health economic study is difficult and expensive, clinicians often have to use intuition and gut feeling to decide on decision thresholds.

Informal setting of decision thresholds can be biased, either by over-emphasising the benefits in comparison with the harms, or vice versa.
