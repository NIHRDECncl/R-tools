Welcome to our tool for understanding, from a patient's perspective, the accuracy and utility of diagnostic tests: how information from a test can usefully inform decisions

This is the second of three apps for learning about and exploring the factors that determine a diagnostic test's accuracy --- no test is 100% avccurate.
Although anyone can use the apps, they have been developed primarily for people who are developing and/or evaluating new tests.
On first sight, evaluation of a diagnostic test seems simple: there are only four possiblities for a test result: true positive (TP), false postive (FP), false negative (FP), and true negative (TN). But there are many measures of test accuracy, and we will mainly focus on two technical measures, sensitivity and specificity, and two measures that make sense to patients and clinicians, positive predictive value (PPV) and negative predictive value.

The key question: is what should do we do with the information from a diagnostic test? The answer is not straightforward, which is why we bult this set of apps, and why it is worth investing some time in using them to explore the factors involved in:
  1. Test accuracy;
    Follow the link     to explore the main technical measures of test accuracy: sensitivity and specificity, true and false postive rates, true and false negative rates.

  2. Clinical accuracy and clinical utility;
    Stay     with this app to learn about and explore the measures of test accuracy and utility that are particularly relevant to patients: predictive values, i.e. the probability given a positive or negative test result.

  3. Uncertainties in assessing test accuracy when the reference standard is not perfect:
    Follow the link to learn how uncertainties in the accuracy of a reference test can bias the measurements of accuracy of new tests.
This set of apps has three shared aims:
  1. Learning
    To enable people to develop an intuitive understanding of terms commonly used in the measurment of test accuracy (e.g. as sensitivity, specificity, and predictive values).

  2. Technical and clinical perspectives
    To contrast the measures of test accuracy that are relevant to technical evaluations (e.g. how well does test B perform in comparison to test A) with those that are relevant to patients and clinical decision-making (e.g. what is the probability of having the condition when it's test is positive, or negative?).

    So, app 1 is about technical accuracy, and app 2 (this app) is about clinical accuracy and clinical utility.

  3. Uncertainty
    To confront uncertainties in the measurements of test accuracy.
    Apps 1 and 2 show uncertainties in evaluation measurements that arise from statistical variation --- the precision of measurements.

    Apps 1 and 2 also show the uncertainties that arise from false postive and false negative results --- the accuracy of the test.

    App 3 allows you to explore the uncertainties that arise in the evaluation of a test when the reference test is not perfect. This is much more complex than might seem at first, as you will discover when you try the app out.

This Shiny app allows you to explore and discover the factors that affect: 
  1. Clinical accuracy: How measures of test accuracy that are important to patients and clinicians depend on:
    • Pre-test probability (i.e. the prevalence of the condition)
    • Sensitivity of the test (i.e. the true poitive rate)
    • Specificity of the test (i.e. the true negative rate)
  2. Clinical utility: How results from a diagnostic test help with clinical decision-making.
    • When a postive result for a rule in decision warrants treat the condition
    • When a negative result for ar ule out decison warrants do not treat the condition
    • When a result (positive or negative) is inconclusive, and may warrant further investigation
