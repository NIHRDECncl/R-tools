Technical notes on conditional survival and prediction intervals

This is not a detailed explantion of the methods used for estimating and displaying conditional survival and prediction intervals, but a brief pointer to sources that informed the development of this app.
The sources are referenced, and linked.

Conditional survival

Conditional survival is one of those ideas that seem to need to be re-discovered repeatedly. A Pubmed search found only 80 articles with "conditional survival" in the title and "prognosis" in the title or abstract. The earliest being in 1995.

Even the most recently published paper using the methodology finds it necessary to explain that:

Some aspects of prognosis are not reflected by cumulative survival estimates. These aspects include information on the time already survived by the patient and the patient's survival compared with the general population. Conditional survival (ie, conditional on having survived a certain period of time already) and relative conditional survival (ie, compared with the general population) do incorporate these aspects. (Baart et al, 2017)

The underlying statistics and graphical presentations of conditional survival data are covered in some detail by (Hieke et al, 2015) in an open access article.

Prediction interval

The fact that prediction for an individual is not the same as prediction for a group, and requires a different measure of uncertainty is even less well known than conditional survival. It is telling that one of the earliest publications on the uncertainties inherent in prediction for individuals was published (open access) in the Journal of Medical Ethics (Henderson and Keiding, 2005). The authors explain why "models and statistical indices can be useful at the group or population level, but that human survival is so uncertain that even the best statistical analysis cannot provide single-number predictions of real use for individual patients".

It is also telling that the concept does not (yet) have a widely used name, at least amongst clinicians.

We use "prediction interval" to mean an interval describing the uncertainty around a prediction for an individual.
The interval is derived from the distribution of individual outcomes, and Henderson and Keiding suggest that the 10th to 90th centiles might be used.

A prediction interval is analogous to the confidence cnterval (or credible interval) for a group.

A way of visualizing the distributions from which prediction intervals can be derived is suggested by (Royston, Parmar, and Altman, 2008).

The challenges of visualizing time-to-event data are further discussed by (Witte, 2008).

On the To Do list for a future version of this app is to include in the margins of the prognosis plots the distributions from which prediction intervals are derived.
There will also be an option to choose the width of the prediction interval. For example, from the 10th to 90th centile to the 5th to 95th centile.
